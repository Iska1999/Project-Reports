Introduction

The following is a summary report of the data analysis performed on NIST’s National Vulnerability Database (NVD) as part of a research internship during Summer 2022. The findings were recorded progressively based on the bi-weekly meetings with Prof. Vidya Mani (https://www.darden.virginia.edu/faculty-research/directory/vidya-mani). Each time section is concluded with a Key Takeaways section which summarizes the main points derived throughout the analysis.

May 20th - June 9th

The first step is to find the most occurring CWE, its parent, and its children. We stop traceback when we arrive at the pillar, and only 1 layer of children are considered so far. The CWE tree can be found at cwe.mitre.org. CWE-79 is the most occurring CWE over the years 2021 (and 2019,2020,2021 as well). Almost 20% of the data has no CWE tag. CWE-79 is the CHILD of CWE-74, and CWE-79 is the child of the pillar CWE-707. CWE-79 is the PARENT of CWE-80 , 81, 83, 84, 85, 87, and 692.

  1. Roughly 20% of the reports don’t have CWE tags, which is problematic for data analysis.

June 10th - June 23rd

Mitre CWE-79 Reports

Between years 2019 - 2021, we have 3,171 CWE-79 reports by Mitre, which composes 53.1% of the total CWE-79 reports. This indicates that 0.28% of the total vendors (1 out of 356) has reported 53.1% of the most occurring CWE tag.

First, it is useful to understand what the CWE-79 vulnerability is. Looking at https://cwe.mitre.org/data/definitions/79.html, we find the following:

  1. By definition, CWE-79 is the improper neutralization of user input before it is placed on the webpage output for other users to use. In laymen terms, I can inject a malicious script that stores passwords from the “Login page” of a website, and other users using this website fall victim to my injection. Ideally, the webpage itself should not allow one user to store private information about another user.

  2. Thus, it makes sense that Mitre is reporting the majority of this vulnerability since it is webpage-based. However, we cannot be sure of this conclusion until we analyze the descriptions and reference links of the CWE-79 reports. Analyzing the description text and reference allows us to see if the categorization is accurate and if the reports are really from standalone websites, or if Mitre is reporting on the behalf of companies and vendors.

Q: Are the CWE tags accurate?

By that we mean: “could the description provide information such that the CWE tag be a child of CWE-79 rather than CWE-79?”. Accurate CWE tags are crucial for developing patches in a timely fashion. An alternate direction would be to analyze the description text of No-info tags to see if they in fact could be tagged.

To do that, we downloaded the most recent version of the 2020 database on four days (June 10th, June 13th, June 16th, June 20th). Next, we read the databases and find the differences between them.
Looking at the entry numbers, no new entry was added throughout this span. One cannot make the assumption that new entries are never added, for a lengthier temporal analysis is required. Q: How and why are scores changed?

A BaseSeverity level change from CRITICAL to LOW for instance implies that a once critical vulnerability has perhaps been patched. Do the data entries include references or information about how and why the scores or BaseSeverity levels changed? Note that in order to perform this analysis, we have to periodically download versions of the database since the current NVD Datafeed only provides access to the most recent version.
To do that, we take CVE-2020-4008 as an example. As shown in the table above, its score decreased between June 10th and June 20th. The goal is to see if certain reference links or information were added to justify the score modification.
Looking at the entries in both June 10th & June 20th, there are no differences (except the score modification). When directed to the reference link, we find that the vendor released a patch, thus explaining the baseScore decrease.
Inspecting at a more granular level, we find that the score decrease is due to changing integrityImpact and availabilityImpact factors from HIGH to LOW.
Thus, we can conclude that scores are actively updated in the NVD if patches or fixes are released.

Analyzing The Severe Reports

Our definition of “severe” is the reports that have a baseSeverity of either HIGH or CRITICAL.

Q: What we can we uncover from the high and critical reports?

Filtering the HIGH and CRITICAL reports, we find that they compose 54% of the NVD (2019 - 2021), where HIGH and CRITICAL constitute 40.5% and 13.5% respectively.

Q: How are the BaseSeverity levels evolving as a function of years?

It seems that the BaseSeverity level distributions are very similar across the years.

Key Takeaways

  1. The NVD is constantly being updated to reflect patches, fixes etc. It must be noted that prior scores are overwritten, so no backlog of changes is kept. Thus, a temporal analysis of a certain vulnerability might be cumbersome.
  2. Severe (HIGH and CRITICAL) levels compose more than half of the NVD reports. This is an interesting finding given what we know about CWE-79. Although CWE-79 is the most prevalent type, it is predominantly of MEDIUM severity.
  3. Performing text analysis to find CWE tags may be of interest.

June 24th - July 7th

Mitre CWE-79 Reports

From this point on, we narrow our focus on CWE-79 vulnerabilities reported by Mitre.

Q: What is the nature of the webpages that have the CWE-79 vulnerabilities?

Are they small blogs, for instance, or major pages run by companies that could have reported the vulnerability themselves?
To find out, we take a look at the references embedded in the CWE-79 reports.

A simple look at the summary of Mitre CWE-79 reference links reveals that they are indeed reported from rather small websites, forums, and blogs. The only prominent entity found within the reference links is GitHub, which is interesting since GitHub does report on its own as can be seen in the Vendors List earlier. Further investigation reveals that the GitHub reference links are mostly references to individuals reporting vulnerabilities in their own applications or projects on their GitHub repositories, and not vulnerabilities in GitHub itself. Recall that GitHub is a provider for software development where developers upload repositories of their code and progress.

Q: Why do different CVE-79 reports by Mitre have the same reference link?

For instance, 66 reports have http://antoniocannito.it/?p=137#uxss as the reference link. Does the link contain information about several unique CVE-79 reports, or are the different reports showing the temporal progress of a single CVE-79 vulnerability?

To further explore, we take several examples of repeated reference links to discover a trend. One exception is enough to negate a hypothesis.
Our first sample is the reports with (http://antoniocannito.it/?p=137#uxss), which happen to be 66 reports. First, we visit the site to find vulnerability descriptions. Next, we investigate the report details. Are the scores different, changing, describing different vulnerabilities?

Q: Are the Mire CWE-79 reports changing with time?

To find out, we download the most recent versions of the datafeeds (July 6th), and perform our comparison analysis. We restrict our scope to 2020 so that websites and blogs have ample time to apply patches and fixes. The comparison analysis returns 0 changes, as the two versions are found to be identical. This is interesting given that we know the NVD is constantly being updated to reflect changes in scores and descriptions, yet no changes were found with the Mitre CWE-79 reports. This could mean that the websites are not patching the vulnerabilities, or they are indeed patching but this is not reported. The second hypothesis is probable given that we know the websites did not report the vulnerabilities themselves, rather Mitre performed the reporting.

Key Takeaways

  1. We focused on CWE-79 reports by Mitre since it is the most common vulnerability by the most common vendor/reporter. We find that the majority of the reports are from small websites or blogs, which makes sense given the nature of the CWE-79 vulnerability discussed last time.
  2. Several unique CVE reports may point to the same reference for various reasons.
  3. No changes in the Mitre CWE-79 reports were observed in year 2020.
